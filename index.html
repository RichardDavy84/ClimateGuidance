Our Data & Methods

At Climate Compass we ground our projections in community‑standard CMIP6 global models and CORDEX regional models. CMIP6 is the latest phase of the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project and provides the scientifically robust foundation for the IPCC’s Sixth Assessment Report【221916107914534†L41-L47】, while CORDEX coordinates regional climate downscaling and supplies ensembles of high‑resolution projections for all terrestrial regions【328286606639560†L18-L25】. For Europe, the EURO‑CORDEX branch increases spatial resolution to about 12 km, allowing finer‑scale representations of climate processes【328286606639560†L56-L59】.

To capture local variability and extremes beyond the reach of coarse models, we employ convective‑permitting simulations and our own statistical downscaling software (KrigR). Convection‑permitting models operate on grid spacings smaller than 4 km and explicitly resolve deep convection; studies show they reduce errors in large‑scale models and provide added value in convective processes, regional extremes and mountainous regions【548565545028849†L317-L323】. Our downscaling optimizes kernels for specific variables and observations, producing layers at the few‑hundred‑metre scale that are ready for decision making.

We supplement these datasets with convective‑permitting and regional datasets where available, then remove biases, quantify uncertainties and tailor outputs to your sector and location. Although climate data are increasingly accessible, decision‑makers often find them hard to interpret and apply; the World Meteorological Organization notes that while climate data may be available, it is often difficult to find, understand and integrate into decision‑making【286123360820464†L169-L179】. Our role is to bridge that gap by curating data, explaining strengths and limitations, and ensuring it is presented in formats that support adaptation and mitigation planning.

Frequently Asked Questions

What climate models do you use?

We base our analyses on the latest CMIP6 global models and the CORDEX regional ensembles – the same scientifically robust datasets used by the IPCC【221916107914534†L41-L47】【328286606639560†L18-L25】. These models provide broad coverage of the climate system, and the CORDEX framework adds high‑resolution regional detail【328286606639560†L56-L59】. We then refine these projections with convective‑permitting simulations and our own KrigR downscaling to deliver local‑scale climate layers.

Why are CMIP6 and CORDEX considered the standard?

CMIP6 is the most recent phase of the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project and underpins the IPCC’s Sixth Assessment Report; its data are scientifically robust and benefit from increased complexity and resolution【221916107914534†L41-L47】. The CORDEX initiative, established by the World Climate Research Programme, provides a coordinated model evaluation and projection framework and serves as an interface between climate simulations and adaptation or mitigation studies【328286606639560†L18-L25】. EURO‑CORDEX simulations, for example, are produced at approximately 12 km resolution【328286606639560†L56-L59】, offering more detailed insights than global models.

What is convective‑permitting modelling, and why does it matter?

Traditional global and regional models operate at grid spacings larger than 10–100 km and rely on parameterizations to represent small‑scale processes such as thunderstorms. Convection‑permitting models operate at grid spacings under 4 km, allowing deep convection to be resolved explicitly. Reviews show that convective‑permitting simulations reduce errors in large‑scale models and add value in representing convective processes, regional extremes and mountainous regions【548565545028849†L317-L323】, making them essential for accurate local‑scale projections.

Why do I need high‑resolution climate data?

Decisions about infrastructure, land management and risk assessment often hinge on local variability. Large‑scale models can have grid spacings exceeding 100 km and may miss micro‑climates or localized extremes【548565545028849†L339-L355】. By combining regional CORDEX simulations and convective‑permitting models with statistical downscaling, we provide fine‑scale layers that capture terrain effects, localized precipitation and temperature extremes, enabling more precise planning.

Why do I need guidance if climate data are publicly available?

The World Meteorological Organization explains that while climate data may be accessible, it is often hard to find, understand and apply; much of the output remains beyond the understanding of end‑users【286123360820464†L169-L179】. Different models have distinct strengths and weaknesses【286123360820464†L224-L231】, and choosing the right ensemble for your problem requires expertise. We help navigate this landscape, interpret uncertainties and deliver information in formats that support actionable decisions.

What deliverables do you provide?

Our deliverables are tailored to client needs. We can deliver high‑resolution data layers for integration into your own analyses, interactive web dashboards for exploring scenarios, narrative memos summarizing key findings and recommendations, or customized reports. The broader climate‑risk industry offers a range of deliverable types, including narrative documents, maps and raw data feeds【974513834506792†L133-L170】; our offerings similarly span these formats, with an emphasis on usability and transparency.

How do your workshops and training help clients?

Our workshops build capacity by teaching participants how to access, assess and combine different climate datasets. We emphasise understanding strengths and limitations of each source, so teams can confidently use data in their own decision‑making. This approach responds to documented challenges: decision‑makers often struggle to choose the best models and to interpret downscaled data【286123360820464†L224-L231】. By the end of a workshop, participants can interpret projections, explore scenarios and communicate findings clearly.
